I'm currently running three businesses while pursuing two degrees at the same time. B.Tech in AI and an MBA at [NMIMS](https://www.nmims.edu/), while building [Horizn Games](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.horizngames.blockpuzzlemaster), managing 350K+ social media followers, and developing [Zode.ai](https://github.com/tusharv2005/zode.ai).

People ask how I do it. Here's the honest answer.

## The Three Businesses

### 1. Horizn Games

Mobile game development studio. First release: Block Puzzle Master on Google Play Store.

**Time commitment:** 15-20 hours/week

- Game development and updates
- User feedback and support
- Marketing and ASO
- Planning future releases

### 2. Content Creation (350K+ Followers)

Faceless social media pages across TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat and YouTube generating 40M+ views.

**Time commitment:** 10-15 hours/week

- Content creation and editing
- Posting and scheduling
- Analytics and optimization
- Brand partnerships

### 3. Zode.ai

Open-source AI coding assistant. Apache-2.0 licensed, runs in VS Code and terminal.

**Time commitment:** 10-15 hours/week

- Feature development
- Community support
- Documentation
- GitHub issues and pull requests

**Total business time:** 35-50 hours/week

## The Two Degrees

### B.Tech in Artificial Intelligence (2023-2027)

Full-time undergraduate program focused on AI, machine learning, and software engineering.

**Time commitment:** 25-30 hours/week

- Classes and lectures
- Assignments and projects
- Labs and practicals
- Exam preparation

### MBA (2026-2028)

Master's in Business Administration integrated with B.Tech program. Classes run concurrently during the day.

**Time commitment:** Integrated with B.Tech schedule

- Classes overlap with B.Tech timing (10 AM - 6 PM)
- Additional case studies and group projects
- Evening networking and seminars when scheduled
- Business plan development

**Total education time:** 40-50 hours/week

## Time Management Reality

### The Math Doesn't Add Up

50 hours for businesses + 50 hours for education = 100 hours/week.

That's 14+ hours a day, 7 days a week. On paper, it's impossible.

In reality, it's a constant juggling act.

### What a Week Actually Looks Like

**Monday-Friday:**

- 7:00 AM - 10:00 AM: Morning work (coding, content planning, emails)
- 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM: Classes (B.Tech AI + MBA integrated program)
- 6:00 PM - 7:00 PM: Dinner + break
- 7:00 PM - 12:00 AM: Deep work (coding, content creation, studying, assignments)

**Saturday:**

- Morning: Deep work on projects (no classes)
- Afternoon: Business planning and development
- Evening: Social/networking or continue work

**Sunday:**

- Morning: Content batching for the week
- Afternoon: Study or business planning
- Evening: Rest (sometimes)

**Reality:** This schedule rarely holds. Something always breaks.

## What Actually Matters

After two years of this, here's what I've learned matters most:

### 1. Prioritization Over Balance

Balance is a myth. Some weeks, school takes 70% of my time (exams). Other weeks, a game launch takes 80%.

The key: **know what matters this week.**

### 2. Systems Over Hustle

I can't work 100 hours/week sustainably. But I can build systems that multiply effort:

- Batch content creation (30 videos in one day)
- Automate social media posting
- Reusable code modules for games
- Documentation that answers recurring questions

### 3. Leverage AI

[Zode.ai](https://github.com/tusharv2005/zode.ai) isn't just a product — it's how I code faster. Claude helps me with assignments. AI tools cut my content editing time in half.

Using AI isn't cheating. It's leverage.

### 4. Strategic Procrastination

Not everything needs to be done now. Some assignments can wait. Some features can ship later. Some content can be scheduled.

Procrastination becomes strategic when you're choosing what to delay, not avoiding everything.

### 5. The 80/20 Rule

80% of results come from 20% of effort. I focus on:

- High-impact business work (features users actually want)
- Critical academic work (core concepts, not busywork)
- Content that actually performs (ignore the rest)

## Sacrifices and Tradeoffs

### What I Gave Up

**Social Life**
Hanging out, parties, casual meetups — mostly gone. I see friends, but it's scheduled, not spontaneous.

**Sleep**
6-7 hours on good nights. 4-5 when deadlines collide. Not sustainable long-term, but it's the reality right now.

**Hobbies**
Reading for fun, gaming (ironically), sports — all minimized. Work and study fill the space.

**Downtime**
Scrolling social media, binge-watching shows, doing nothing — these barely exist. Every hour has a purpose.

### What I Kept

**Health**
I still exercise (30 min/day). Skipping workouts makes everything harder.

**Family Time**
Dinner with family most nights. It's non-negotiable.

**Learning**
Even outside coursework, I read and learn. It's not a sacrifice — it's part of the work.

## The Stress

Let's be honest: it's a lot.

### The Good Stress

- Launching a game and seeing downloads
- Hitting 1M views on a video
- Shipping a Zode.ai feature people love
- Acing an exam you prepared for

### The Bad Stress

- Deadlines colliding (exam + game launch + MBA project)
- Bugs in production while studying for finals
- Content not performing after hours of work
- Feeling behind in everything, all the time

### How I Manage It

- **Exercise** — clears my head
- **Breaks** — even 10 minutes helps
- **Perspective** — this is temporary, not forever
- **Community** — talking to people who get it

## Is It Worth It?

### Financially

Not yet. The businesses generate income, but not enough to justify the effort. I'm investing in future returns.

### Career-wise

Absolutely. B.Tech gives me technical depth. MBA gives me business understanding. The businesses give me real-world experience.

Together, they compound.

### Personally

Some days, yes. Building things I care about, learning constantly, seeing progress — it feels meaningful.

Other days, no. Exhausted, stressed, questioning if it's too much.

The answer changes weekly.

## What I'd Tell Someone Considering This

**Don't do it if:**

- You're looking for work-life balance (it doesn't exist here)
- You need immediate financial returns (this is a long game)
- You're not okay with sacrificing your 20s for building

**Do it if:**

- You're genuinely excited about what you're building
- You see education as investment, not obligation
- You're okay with delayed gratification
- You want to compress 10 years of learning into 5

## The Long Game

I'm not doing this forever. But right now, in my early 20s, I have:

- Energy to work long hours
- No major responsibilities (no kids, no mortgage)
- Access to education and resources
- Ideas I want to bring to life

In 5 years, I'll have:

- Two degrees (B.Tech AI, MBA)
- Multiple businesses with proven traction
- A portfolio of shipped projects
- Network and reputation built over time

That's the bet. Whether it pays off — I'll know in a few years.

For now, I'm building. Three businesses, two degrees, one life.
