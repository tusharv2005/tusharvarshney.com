I'm running three businesses solo: [Horizn Games](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.horizngames.blockpuzzlemaster), content creation (350K+ followers), and [Zode.ai](https://github.com/tusharv2005/zode.ai). Here's everything I wish someone had told me before I started.

## Legal Setup

### Business Structure

**What I did:** Started as a sole proprietor (informal). Minimal paperwork, easy to begin.

**What I should have done earlier:** Registered as a proper entity. In India, options are:

- **Sole Proprietorship** — Simplest, but no legal separation between you and the business
- **One Person Company (OPC)** — Limited liability, more credibility
- **Private Limited** — Best for scaling, investor-ready, but more compliance

**My advice:** If you're testing an idea, start informal. Once you have revenue or paying customers, formalize it. The legal protection is worth the paperwork.

### Contracts & Agreements

**What I learned the hard way:**

1. **Always have a contract** — Even with friends. Especially with friends.
2. **Terms of service matter** — For apps and platforms. Use templates, customize them.
3. **IP ownership** — Make sure you own what you create. If you hire someone, get it in writing.
4. **NDAs are overrated** — Most people won't steal your idea. Execution matters more.

**Resources:**

- [India Filings](https://www.indiafilings.com/) for company registration
- [LegalZoom India](https://www.legalzoom.com/) for templates
- Local CA for tax setup

### Licenses & Compliance

For game development (Google Play, App Store):

- Developer accounts ($25 for Google, $99/year for Apple)
- Privacy policy (required for data collection)
- GDPR compliance if you have European users
- Payment gateway KYC (Razorpay, Stripe)

**Tip:** Set up compliance early. Retroactive compliance is painful.

## Financial Management

### Separating Business and Personal

**Mistake I made:** Used personal account for business transactions. Tax time was a nightmare.

**What you should do:**

- Open a separate business bank account
- Use separate credit cards for business expenses
- Track every transaction (even small ones)

### Accounting Basics

You don't need an accountant on day one. But you need a system:

**Tools I use:**

- **Google Sheets** — Simple expense tracking
- **Zoho Books** — When revenue grows (free tier available)
- **Local CA** — For tax filing (worth the cost)

**What to track:**

- Revenue (by source: games, content, freelance)
- Expenses (tools, ads, services)
- Profit/loss monthly
- Tax obligations (GST, Income Tax)

### Pricing Your Work

**Content creation pricing:**

- Start with your time cost: (desired monthly income / working hours) = hourly rate
- Add 30-50% for taxes, overhead
- Charge per deliverable, not per hour (when possible)

**Software/products:**

- Research competitor pricing
- Consider value, not just cost
- Start higher — easier to discount than raise prices

### Taxes (India Context)

**GST:** Required if turnover > ₹20 lakhs (₹10 lakhs for services)

**Income Tax:** File ITR for all business income

**TDS:** Deducted on freelance payments (claim it back when filing)

**Tip:** Set aside 30% of revenue for taxes. Better to have extra than owe money you don't have.

## Mental Models

### The 80/20 Rule

80% of results come from 20% of effort. Apply everywhere:

- **Features:** 20% of features drive 80% of usage
- **Content:** 20% of posts get 80% of engagement
- **Customers:** 20% of users generate 80% of revenue

Focus on the 20% that matters.

### Minimum Viable Everything

**MVP for products:** Ship the smallest thing that delivers value.

**MVP for content:** One decent post beats ten mediocre drafts.

**MVP for marketing:** One channel done well beats five channels done poorly.

Perfectionism kills momentum. Ship, learn, iterate.

### Compounding Over Sprinting

Solo founder trap: trying to do everything at once.

Better approach: small daily progress compounds.

- 1% better every day = 37x better in a year
- Consistency beats intensity

**Example:** I batched 30 videos in one day. That content lasted a month. One intense session, 30 days of compounding results.

### Optionality > Optimization

Early on, keep options open:

- Don't lock into one platform (diversify)
- Don't over-optimize unproven ideas
- Test multiple approaches, double down on what works

Optimization comes after you've found what works.

### Leverage

Solo founders need leverage to compete:

**Capital leverage:** Money (ads, tools, services)
**Labor leverage:** People (contractors, VAs)
**Code leverage:** Automation (scripts, AI, tools)
**Media leverage:** Content (1 video reaches millions)

Focus on media and code leverage first — they scale without proportional cost.

## When to Bring in Help

### Signs You Need Help

1. **You're the bottleneck** — Work is piling up, quality is dropping
2. **You're outside your zone of genius** — Spending time on tasks others do better/faster
3. **Opportunity cost is high** — The thing you could be doing is worth more than the cost of help
4. **You're burned out** — Mental health matters more than saving money

### What to Outsource First

**Start with low-skill, time-consuming tasks:**

- Video editing (if you're creating content)
- Customer support emails
- Social media scheduling
- Basic graphic design

**Keep these in-house longer:**

- Core product decisions
- Strategy and vision
- Customer conversations (early on)
- Anything that defines your brand

### How to Hire as a Solo Founder

**Contractors > Employees** (initially)

- Lower commitment
- Pay for output, not hours
- Easy to scale up/down

**Where to find people:**

- **Upwork** / **Fiverr** for one-off tasks
- **Twitter** / **LinkedIn** for specialists
- **Referrals** from other founders

**Red flags:**

- Can't show previous work
- Vague timelines
- Poor communication
- Asks for full payment upfront

**Green flags:**

- Clear portfolio
- Asks good questions
- Suggests improvements
- Transparent about process

### When to Bring in a Co-Founder

**Good reasons:**

- Complementary skills (you're technical, they're business)
- Shared vision and values
- Someone you've worked with before
- Long-term commitment aligned

**Bad reasons:**

- You're lonely (join communities instead)
- You need motivation (that's a you problem)
- They have an idea (ideas are cheap, execution matters)
- Pressure from others

**My take:** I've stayed solo intentionally. Co-founders are like marriage — choose carefully or stay single.

## Mistakes I Made

**1. Waiting too long to charge**
Built for free for months. Should have charged earlier, even a small amount. Paying customers give better feedback than free users.

**2. Not tracking metrics early**
Didn't measure what mattered. Now I track everything: revenue, engagement, time spent. You can't improve what you don't measure.

**3. Overbuilding before validation**
Built features no one asked for. Should have talked to users first, built second.

**4. Saying yes to everything**
Took on projects that didn't align with goals. Every yes is a no to something else. Be selective.

**5. Neglecting marketing**
Built great products, assumed people would find them. They didn't. Marketing is half the job.

## What I'd Tell My Past Self

**Month 1:** Just start. Your first version will be bad. Ship it anyway.

**Month 6:** Focus on one thing. You think you're multitasking. You're not. Pick one project, make it work, then add more.

**Year 1:** Build in public. Share your journey. The audience you build becomes your unfair advantage.

**Year 2:** Systemize everything. If you do it twice, document it. If you do it three times, automate it.

**Year 3:** Hire sooner than comfortable. The cost of your time is higher than you think.

## Final Thoughts

Being a solo founder is hard. You're the CEO, developer, marketer, support, and janitor. But it's also the most direct path to building exactly what you want, how you want it.

The trade-offs are real: less social life, more stress, constant uncertainty. But the upsides — autonomy, ownership, learning — make it worth it.
